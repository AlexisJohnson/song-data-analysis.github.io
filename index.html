What makes a song popular?¶

Most people listen to music in some form. It can range from hearing a catchy tune while at a grocery store, to having a devoted playlist for every emotion possibly felt throughout your day. But what makes a song widely enjoyable to a larger audience of people? What exactly makes a song popular? Imagine you are an artist trying to make the next biggest hit, and the goal is for your next song to make it to the Billboard End of the Year list. This exploratory data analysis will attempt to answer for what makes a song popular, and provide some insights for the artist.

Using the search feature in the spotify api can return a lot of information about a given track, such as countries where it is available, all associated artists and links to their profile on spotify, popularity ranking, etc. Because of this we will now use the spotify search to get trackids and artistids. We can use the artist id to get the genre,and the track id to get features present within the actual song, such as "danceability" and "energy". Since we are searching by keyword, this can make the search results prone to errors, especially in a larger dataset like this (i.e. getting a live, acoustic, remix version of the original song we are searching for).

We cannot completely filter out remixes, as sometimes the remixes are what is more popular (i.e. Finesse Remix Bruno Mars ft. Cardi B). Therefore we will have a marker denoting whether the song is a remix for manual inspection. We will also search songs that have words such as 'Instrumental', 'Live', and 'Acoustic'. This won't completely filter out false search results, so in addition we will have a low popularity marker if the track popularity score is less than 40. A lower popularity score would increase the likelihood for a false search result, as all of the songs we are searching should in theory would have a higher popularity score.

Finally we will parse the artist by only focusing on the main artist (if there is a feature/other people in the track)

In [ ]:
def parse_artist(content):
    for split_token in [" & ", " \\ ", " feat ", " featuring ", " and "]:
        content = content.partition(split_token)[0]
    return content

Through this we can minimize the false results we will get when searching. We have empty lists which we will aggregate with values for each call to the Spotify API

        artist_ids.append(np.nan)

def search2(dataframe):
    ids = []
    artist_ids = []
    check = [] #I will add all song titles to check as a sanity check
    amt_features = []
    for row in dataframe.iterrows():
        search = row[1][0].replace("\"", "") + " " + parse_artist(row[1][1])
        results = spotify.search(q=search,type='track', limit=1)
        res = results['tracks']['items']
        if len(res) == 0:
            ids.append(np.nan)
            check.append(np.nan)
            artist_ids.append(np.nan)
            amt_features.append(np.nan)
        else:
            ids.append(res[0]['id'])
            check.append(res[0]['name'])
            artist_ids.append(res[0]['artists'][0]['id'])
            amt_features.append(len(res[0]['artists']) - 1)
    dataframe['track_ids'] = ids
    dataframe['artist_ids'] = artist_ids       
    dataframe['check'] = check
    dataframe['amt_features'] = amt_features

Acousticness

  • A confidence measure from 0.0 to 1.0 of whether the track is acoustic. 1.0 represents high confidence the track is acoustic.

Danceability

  • Danceability describes how suitable a track is for dancing based on a combination of musical elements including tempo, rhythm stability, beat strength, and overall regularity. A value of 0.0 is least danceable and 1.0 is most danceable.

Energy

  • Energy is a measure from 0.0 to 1.0 and represents a perceptual measure of intensity and activity. Typically, energetic tracks feel fast, loud, and noisy. For example, death metal has high energy, while a Bach prelude scores low on the scale. Perceptual features contributing to this attribute include dynamic range, perceived loudness, timbre, onset rate, and general entropy.

Instrumentalness

  • Predicts whether a track contains no vocals. "Ooh" and "aah" sounds are treated as instrumental in this context. Rap or spoken word tracks are clearly "vocal". The closer the instrumentalness value is to 1.0, the greater likelihood the track contains no vocal content. Values above 0.5 are intended to represent instrumental tracks, but confidence is higher as the value approaches 1.0.

Liveness

  • Detects the presence of an audience in the recording. Higher liveness values represent an increased probability that the track was performed live. A value above 0.8 provides strong likelihood that the track is live.

Loudness

  • The overall loudness of a track in decibels (dB). Loudness values are averaged across the entire track and are useful for comparing relative loudness of tracks. Loudness is the quality of a sound that is the primary psychological correlate of physical strength (amplitude). Values typically range between -60 and 0 db. mode

Speechiness

  • Speechiness detects the presence of spoken words in a track. The more exclusively speech-like the recording (e.g. talk show, audio book, poetry), the closer to 1.0 the attribute value. Values above 0.66 describe tracks that are probably made entirely of spoken words. Values between 0.33 and 0.66 describe tracks that may contain both music and speech, either in sections or layered, including such cases as rap music. Values below 0.33 most likely represent music and other non-speech-like tracks.

You can get more informtion regarding what each of these keys mean at https://developer.spotify.com/documentation/web-api/reference/#/operations/get-several-audio-features

Insights and Limitations The most important features, according to classification, is the artist making the track themselves, danceability, and tempo. These can be great insights for one trying to understand the general direction music is headed at. However these insights can be pretty limited as to what makes a song popular. There are many factors that go into the popularity of a song that go beyond the scope of simply the artist themselves, such as with production, songwriters, social influence, and the record label itself. Labels for instance pay radio stations and streamers to push out music, so that in itself is also a strategy that isn't present in the data. There can also be limitations with the way that the data has been collected. While I am sure the majority of my data is correct, Spotify Search has the tendency of poducing bad results, so that can also be another factor to note. Finally, artists nowdays are quite versatile, and many expand further than just one genre. Since there was no genre attached to the track, I attached the first genre associated with the artist which isn't always reflective of the song.
